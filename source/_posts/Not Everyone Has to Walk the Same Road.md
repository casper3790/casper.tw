# Not Everyone Has to Walk the Same Road
There was a period in my life when almost everyone around me was preparing for the same thing.
By the second semester of my second year of high school, most people had already started thinking about their final year of high school, entrance exams, and the future they were expected to pursue.
The atmosphere was clear: study harder, prepare earlier, and stay on the usual track.
But I did not walk that road in the same way.
During that time, I met a teacher who fought alongside me.
He was not just someone who taught in a classroom. He was someone who noticed that I was trying to find a different direction, and instead of pulling me back, he chose to stand with me.
I still remember one of the moments that changed me.
There was a cybersecurity workshop opportunity, and to most people, it might have looked like just another activity.
But to me, it felt like a door opening.
I rushed toward it almost instinctively, as if I had finally found something worth chasing with everything I had.
At that time, I was like a hunting dog that had gone mad — as long as it was related to cybersecurity, whether it was a workshop, a training session, or a competition, I would throw myself at it without hesitation.
I was not chasing it because it was easy.
I was chasing it because, for the first time, I felt something inside me come alive.
Around that time, that teacher said something to me that I still remember:

“If you really believe this is your road, then don’t walk it halfway.”

That sentence stayed with me.
Because from then on, this was no longer just curiosity.
It became a choice.
When I entered my final year of high school, the difference between my path and everyone else’s became even more obvious.
While many people around me were buried in textbooks, mock exams, and application materials, my nights often looked very different.
There were nights when the school day was already over, everyone else had gone back to preparing for tests, and I was still sitting in front of my screen, developing websites line by line, fixing layouts, testing ideas, rewriting content, or staying up late to work on my paper.
The room would be quiet, the screen would be the brightest thing in front of me, and the clock would keep moving long past the time I should have been asleep.
Sometimes I was coding.
Sometimes I was writing.
Sometimes I was stuck for hours on a single problem.
But even in that exhaustion, I knew I was building something that belonged to me.
That path was never a shortcut.
It demanded discipline, obsession, and the ability to keep going when nobody could guarantee that it would work out.
When everyone around you is walking in one direction, choosing another means carrying more doubt, more pressure, and more responsibility by yourself.
But I kept going.
And then, by the second semester of my final year of high school, something became real.
I knew I already had a university I could go to.
I still remember the feeling.
It was not the kind of happiness that explodes all at once.
It was more like finally being able to breathe after holding that breath for a very long time.
It was relief, yes.
But more than that, it was proof.
Proof that this road had not been meaningless.
Proof that all those late nights, all the workshops I chased, all the competitions I joined, and all the projects I kept building had taken me somewhere real.
Proof that even if my path did not look standard, it was still a path that could lead forward.
This story is not about saying that studying does not matter.
It is not about telling people to stop reading books or stop preparing for exams.
What I want to say is something much simpler:
Studying is not the only road.
For some people, there are other paths worth walking.
Paths built through projects, competitions, technical practice, research, persistence, and the courage to keep moving even when your direction looks different from everyone else’s.
I do not think everyone should copy my path.
But I do believe that not everyone has to walk the same road.
Sometimes, the most important thing is not whether your path looks standard.
It is whether you are truly willing to fight for it.
And for me, that was the road I chose.
