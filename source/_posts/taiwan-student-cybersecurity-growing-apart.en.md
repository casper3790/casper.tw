# Is Taiwan's Student Cybersecurity Community Really Growing Apart?

> This article only represents my personal experience and observations.  
> The activities, competitions, and events mentioned here are shared from how I felt during my own participation. They do not represent the position of any organization, unit, or group.  
> If readers see things differently, rational discussion is welcome.

This question has bothered me for a long time.

From my first security competition, to learning Cybersecurity, preparing certifications, joining CTF, and leading junior students into competitions, I have been thinking about the same thing:

Is Taiwan's student Cybersecurity community still suitable for beginners to enter?

I am not trying to criticize any organization, nor am I trying to deny anyone's effort.

On the contrary, I want to write these feelings down precisely because I like Cybersecurity.

---

## From My First Competition to Now

Over the past few years, I went from knowing almost nothing to gradually encountering CTF, Web Security, digital forensics, Red Team, Blue Team, and eventually leading junior students into competitions.

When I first encountered security, I thought the field was cool.

You could use technology to find problems, analyze vulnerabilities, solve challenges, and protect systems through your own ability.

Back then, I believed that as long as I worked hard, I could find my place in this community.

But after walking this far, I have begun to realize that things are not that simple.

---

## The Northern Regional Round of the National Skills Competition

In 2026, I brought junior students to the Northern regional round of the National Skills Competition for the Cybersecurity category.

On the first day, after the written exam, they came out and told me:

> Senior, the questions inside felt very similar to the Golden Shield competition.

At the time, I thought that was probably a good sign. Maybe there was a chance to advance.

As I expected, we successfully made it into the regional round.

On the day of the regional round, the morning session was digital forensics.

During the lunch break, I saw their expressions and knew something was not right.

They said the problems were hard.

At that moment, all I could tell them was that it was okay, and that there would still be Red Team practice in the afternoon, so they could bring their state back.

At lunch, I also talked with other participants.

After chatting, I realized that many strong competitors were there, including students from NTU, NCCU, some from NYCU, and even people with national-level experience.

For students participating in a competition of that scale for the first time, that pressure was actually very heavy.

After the afternoon competition ended, we did not qualify for the national round.

The junior students were very upset.

All I could tell them was:

> Whether the result was good or bad, this was still experience.  
> Being willing to stand on the competition floor is already a brave thing.

---

## My First CTF: Was It Really a Beginner's First CTF?

Later, we joined My First CTF, organized by AIS3.

For me and another junior student, it was a good training opportunity.

We walked into NYCU's campus together to participate in the competition.

At the time, I had expectations for the event.

Based on the name, My First CTF should have been a first CTF for beginners.

So I originally thought the challenges would be more suitable for beginners than HTB or other online target machines.

But in reality, the problems were much harder than expected.

I am not saying this from behind the scenes after the fact. On that day, many participants really were stuck on even the first challenge.

I am not saying challenges cannot be hard.

CTF should be challenging by nature.

But when a competition is called My First CTF, I believe it should also carry the responsibility of guiding beginners.

---

## How the Post-Competition Explanations Felt

After the competition ended, before the awards ceremony, the challenge authors came out to explain the problems.

I understand that the speakers may have wanted to explain the challenges in a lighter way, or simply avoid making the atmosphere too serious.

But some of the explanation style at the time made some participants uncomfortable.

Especially when submitted flags were brought out as punchlines, what some people felt was not humor, but being mocked.

For me, maybe I could still handle it.

But for beginners joining CTF for the first time, that feeling may be amplified.

Afterward, when I talked with other participants, some people mentioned similar feelings.

What they wanted to say was simple:

> Explain the challenge, then explain it.  
> But participants should not feel like they are being used as jokes.

Maybe the organizers or challenge authors had no bad intentions.

But for beginners, the feeling was real.

---

## After the AIS3 Pre-exam, I Started to Waver

Originally, I thought the matter had passed, and I would just return to my usual routine of studying security and joining competitions.

Later, I participated in the AIS3 Pre-exam online competition.

This experience completely reduced part of my enthusiasm for charging forward in Cybersecurity.

After the competition ended, the organizers required participants to submit a write-up.

I thought that since I had joined, I should organize it seriously.

So I spent more than an hour organizing my solution process and submitted it to the official side.

I originally thought that was the end of it.

Until the day the summer camp results were released, I opened Gmail and found that I was not admitted.

At first, I was confused.

Because my rank was around the forties.

Later, I asked other friends and found that some people ranked around 170 had been admitted. I also heard that someone who did not submit a write-up had been admitted.

At that moment, I really could not swallow it.

So I wrote to ask the organizers, AIS3 official.

The general meaning of the reply I received was:

> We also want to confirm whether there was any overlooked step in the process, which led to students like that being admitted.

When I saw that response, my first thought was:

If there may really have been an overlooked step, shouldn't the entire process be rechecked?

Why was the focus on confirming whether certain admitted people had submitted write-ups?

I am not saying I absolutely had to be admitted.

Admission is never guaranteed.

But if there are real doubts in the process, what participants most hope to see is transparency, fairness, and a complete recheck.

Not the feeling that the matter simply passed like that.

---

## What I Worry About Is Beginners

For who I am now, these things may just be experiences.

But what if this happened to a student who had only just encountered Cybersecurity?

What if it was their first time joining a competition, first time preparing seriously, first time writing a write-up, and first time feeling full of expectations for this field?

And what they received was confusion, frustration, and opacity.

Would they still be willing to continue?

That is what I am truly worried about.

Some people may say:

> Cybersecurity is hard by nature.  
> If you cannot endure it, then you are not suited for it.

But I do not fully agree.

Technical difficulty is one thing.

Whether the environment is willing to let beginners stay is another.

---

## A Reflection from a Teacher

Once, when I attended a security-related meeting, I asked a teacher:

> If a student wants to enter the Cybersecurity community now, what advice would you give them?

I originally thought I would hear the same kind of answer as before.

For example:

Play more CTF, join more competitions, and accumulate more competition experience.

But the teacher's answer left a strong impression on me.

He said:

> Today's Cybersecurity community is no longer like the old one.  
> Many students only learn how to attack others, which is Red Team work.  
> But what the industry really lacks now is Blue Team talent: people who understand defense, operations, monitoring, and system protection.

That conversation made me think for a long time.

We spend a lot of time studying vulnerabilities, penetration, and attack techniques.

But is the essence of Cybersecurity really attack?

Or is it protection?

If everyone wants to be Red Team, who will be Blue Team?

If everyone wants to find vulnerabilities, who will protect the systems?

Maybe this is also one reason I later began touching robotics, medical systems, AI, and other real application fields.

Because I began to realize that the value of technology is not only proving what you can break.

It is also what you can protect, improve, and create.

---

## If Someone Asked Me Why Fewer Students Stay in Cybersecurity

If someone asked me today:

> Why are there fewer and fewer people in Taiwan's student Cybersecurity community?

My answer may be different from many people's.

Many people think it is because security is too hard.

Many people think it is because students lack patience.

But I do not think the real problem is entirely that.

The student security community today seems to have only two types of people left.

One type is people who are already very strong.

The other type is beginners who have only just encountered security.

The group that disappears most easily is exactly the group in the middle.

They are interested in Cybersecurity.

They are willing to spend time learning.

They are willing to join activities.

They are willing to stay up late researching challenges.

But when they encounter frustration, confusion, and an unfriendly environment again and again, their passion is slowly worn down.

They do not necessarily leave because they hate Cybersecurity.

Often, it is the opposite. They leave because they liked Cybersecurity too much.

But when they tried to understand the community more deeply, they found themselves always standing outside the door.

---

## The Problem Is Not That No One Is Interested, But That No One Stays

I think the biggest problem in the security community has never been a lack of interested people.

It is the lack of an environment that makes those people willing to stay.

If a community knows where the problems are, but no one is willing to change;

if more activities are held every year and more resources are invested, but beginners' experiences are not truly improved;

then the result may still be the same.

People come in.

People are disappointed.

Then people leave.

I believe these problems did not happen in one or two days.

They accumulated year after year, causing many people to slowly leave the security community.

---

## After AI Emerged, Cybersecurity Needs to Rethink Its Position

AI is developing rapidly now.

More and more students are moving into AI, data science, machine learning, and related fields.

There is nothing wrong with that.

Everyone has the right to choose their own direction.

But I often wonder:

If those people who were once passionate about Cybersecurity had received more guidance, a little more tolerance, and a few more people willing to lead them,

would they still be in this community today?

I do not know the answer.

But I know that what truly makes people leave is often not the technology itself.

It is losing the original passion little by little while chasing the technology.

---

## Closing Thoughts

To this day, I still like Cybersecurity.

I still play CTF.

I still lead junior students.

I still join activities.

But I have also started thinking:

Are we cultivating more people with passion for Cybersecurity,

or are we pushing them away from the community through frustration after frustration?

I do not hate Cybersecurity.

I am not trying to deny anyone's effort.

I only hope that in the future, more people who enter this field can stay.

This article is only my personal experience and feelings.

It does not represent the position of any organization, unit, or group.

It is not targeted at any unit or any person.

After walking through these years, I have simply started to feel:

Taiwan's student Cybersecurity community may really be growing farther apart.
