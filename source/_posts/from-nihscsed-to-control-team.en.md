> Some stories do not end.  
> They simply continue in a different form.

## Starting as a Small Participant

I was not someone who stood at the front from the beginning.

At first, I was only a person slowly exploring and slowly participating in the community. From being a small participant, to gradually taking part in more work and carrying more responsibility, I eventually reached the point where I helped create the **NIHSCED IWHC white-hat security community**.

That period was important to me.

It did not only introduce me to Cybersecurity. It also let me understand, for the first time, what kind of force a student community could become.

It can bring scattered people together.  
It can give students who are interested in security a place to discuss, learn, and build.  
It can also let us start trying to define our own direction before we are fully mature.

Back then, I never imagined I would eventually arrive at the position I held later.

I simply felt that if there were people at school who wanted to learn security, then we should have a place where we could keep walking together.  
If some people could not find a direction, then we should try to build an entry point that let them begin.

I once believed the community would keep moving forward in its original shape.

But later, I gradually understood that some things do not disappear.  
People simply begin walking toward different roads.

## Not Letting the Community Disappear, But Knowing When Paths Diverge

I have never believed that NIHSCED IWHC was ended by me.

It did not disappear.  
It simply reached a moment when the direction my partners wanted to take and the direction I wanted to take began to differ.

Everyone has their own goals, choices, and things they want to invest in.  
As the general coordinator, I could not use only my own thoughts to require everyone to keep walking the same road.

Some people wanted to keep digging deeper into security.  
Some began putting more focus on school advancement, life, or other fields.  
Some may simply have walked with us during one specific period of life.

I know that sometimes, the truly responsible choice is not to force everyone to stay in the same place.  
It is to acknowledge that our directions are different, and then let each person move toward the place they believe in.

So I chose to accept it.

Not because I gave up on the community.  
But because I knew it was time for our paths to diverge.

To me, that was not a failure.  
It felt more like a journey reaching a turning point.

And I had to ask myself:

Where should I put my energy next?

## Returning to the Control Engineering Department

In the end, I returned to the place where my teacher was.  
That was the **Control Engineering department at Neihu Vocational High School**.

To me, that place was not only the department I studied in.  
It was more like the starting point where I began rethinking student teams, technical continuity, and future direction.

At that time, the Control Engineering department already had many different competition teams and project groups.

Some students were working on robotics.  
Some were working on mechatronics.  
Some were building control-related projects.  
Some were working hard in different competitions and projects.

But these forces were scattered.

Every team worked hard, but there was no clearer direction connecting them.  
Every competitor was moving forward in their own field, but the department still lacked a core that could connect these stories.

I began to think:

If these people were not only working separately, but could be connected, would the Control Engineering department become different?

If robotics, mechatronics, project groups, competitors, and students from different technical directions could be integrated into a more complete team, would junior students see more possibilities?

So I began discussing this with the advisors of different competition teams.

Through repeated conversations, we slowly arrived at a shared idea:

**We wanted to build the Control Engineering department at Neihu Vocational High School into a more distinctive competition team with a clearer direction.**

## Control Engineering Is Not Just Textbook Learning

I want the next group of junior students to know that entering the Control Engineering department does not mean they can only learn what is written in textbooks.

Textbooks matter.  
Fundamentals matter.  
But the most valuable part of Control Engineering should not stop inside the textbook.

We can integrate competition teams, project groups, hands-on ability, and competition experience.

Students from different fields can have opportunities to build work together.  
To finish projects together.  
To present in different cities and counties.  
To let more people see the strength of the Control Engineering department.

I do not want the department to be only a place for classes, exams, and homework.

I hope it can be a place where students find direction.  
A place where students know they can build real things.  
A place where every person willing to work hard has a chance to be seen.

Technology should not only be an answer on an exam paper.

It should be built.  
It should be demonstrated.  
It should be brought to competition venues.  
It should be brought to project presentations.  
It can also become the starting point where a student finds confidence and direction.

## From General Coordinator to Someone Pushing the Department Forward

In the past, I was the general coordinator of NIHSCED IWHC.

I was responsible for the community, for gathering people, and for giving everyone a space to learn security together.

But now, I am gradually shifting into another role.

I no longer only want to run a security community.  
I have started wanting to push a department, a team, and even a new kind of student culture forward.

I want to integrate the scattered competition teams.  
I want project groups and competitors to stop moving only on their own.  
I want junior students in the Control Engineering department to know that they can actually have bigger dreams.

To me, that is a major shift.

From being a community coordinator to becoming someone who wants to lead the Control Engineering department toward another level.  
This is not a change of title. It is a change of responsibility.

Before, I thought about how a community should operate.  
Now, I think about how students in a department can be seen.

Before, I thought about how to gather interested people together.  
Now, I think about how to let the next class of junior students know, even before they enter, that the department is more than textbooks and exams.

I began to realize that what I carry is not only my own story.  
It also includes the paths that future junior students may walk.

## I Also Lost Some Things Along the Way

But along this road, I did not only gain things.

As I put time into the community, competitions, projects, the website, and the Control Engineering competition team, I also lost many things I could have had.

Time with friends.  
Ordinary after-school moments spent chatting, playing, and making memories.  
Days when I did not have to constantly think about the next step or keep pushing myself forward.

Sometimes I wonder whether life would have been easier if I had not chosen this path.

Would I have had more time with friends?  
More time to rest?  
More time to keep my youth in a simpler place, like an ordinary student?

But I also know these choices were not meaningless.

Because these tradeoffs slowly shaped who I am now.

I lost some time with friends, but I gained more responsibility, more experience, and a direction I truly want to protect.

This is not a very romantic thing.

Sometimes it is lonely.  
Sometimes it is tiring.  
Sometimes I even question whether the things I am doing have any meaning.

But if this road can help future junior students feel a little less lost, help students in the department have a little more direction, and help more people understand that they are not only studying, but can truly build things, then I am willing to admit that these losses are also part of my choice.

Because I know some roads are not meant to be easy.

But if this road can leave something behind, and let the people who come later walk a little more clearly, then it is no longer only my road.

## I Do Not Want Junior Students to Chase Blindly

I am not doing these things to make myself look impressive.  
Nor am I trying to prove that I am more capable than anyone else.

I only hope that my own story can help future junior students avoid some of the detours I went through.

I have been lost before.  
I have not known where I was going.  
I once thought I could only work hard within a path that had already been defined.

But later, I slowly understood that students should not blindly chase a label.

They should not rush forward only for the words "national university of science and technology."  
They should not hand their life to a road everyone calls correct before they even know what they want to do.

I am not saying advancement is unimportant.  
I am not saying getting into a good school has no value.

What I want to say is:

**If a student has no goal, no dream, and nothing they truly want to complete, then even if they are walking on a road that looks correct, they may still be moving blindly.**

I hope every student who enters the Control Engineering department at Neihu Vocational High School can find a direction that belongs to them.

Whether that is robotics, Cybersecurity, mechatronics, project building, or some field that has not yet been seen.

As long as they are willing to work hard, I hope the department can become a place that supports them as they move forward.

## What I Want to Leave Is Not a Title, But a Road

To me, the Control Engineering competition team is not something meant to be used as a title.

It should be a system that can continue.  
An entry point that lets junior students know where they can go.  
A platform that connects different technical fields.

I do not want this to stop with my class.

If it is only a brief moment of enthusiasm, it will disappear quickly.  
If it is only one person's idea, it will be hard to keep going for long.

I hope it can become a form of continuity.

Let the next class of junior students know that Control Engineering is not limited to a single direction.  
Let the competition training experience accumulated by teachers be seen, organized, and continued.  
Let every student who is willing to work hard have the chance to join a team that can truly support their growth.

What I want to leave behind is not a title.  
It is a road.

A road that the people after us can keep walking.

## Our Story Has Never Reached a Full Stop

To me, NIHSCED IWHC is not a door that was closed.  
It is a road I once walked.

That road taught me how to gather people, organize a community, and carry responsibility.  
It also taught me that a student can actually do something much bigger than they once imagined.

And now, I have brought those experiences back to the Control Engineering department.

I hope the Control Engineering competition team can become the next beginning.

A place that can train, compete, build projects, continue knowledge, and help junior students find dreams.

This is not the end.  
It is a new stage.

I went from a small participant in a security community to the community's general coordinator.  
Then from a coordinator, I gradually became someone who wants to push the Control Engineering department forward.

Along this road, I lost direction and found it again.  
I left one road and stepped onto another.

But I believe none of these experiences were wasted.

Because our story has never reached a full stop.  
It has simply moved to another place and started again.
